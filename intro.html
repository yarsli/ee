Quantum Mechanics

Quantum mechanics is the study of the universe on its smallest scales, a realm where the rules of everyday life begin to dissolve... Particles can act like waves, systems can exist in multiple states at once, and all this isn’t guesswork—it’s grounded in decades of confirmed experiments.

Wavefunction & Hilbert Space

The wavefunction contains everything known about a quantum system. It doesn’t deliver a single answer, like “Where is the particle?” Instead, it describes every possible outcome—and the probabilities of each. This lives within an abstract space called Hilbert space, a structure encompassing every possible state of a system.

Measurement & Collapse

When we measure a system, the wavefunction “collapses” to a single outcome. Before measurement, the system is in a superposition of possibilities. This isn’t just about limitations in perception—it’s how nature behaves.

Core Features

Uncertainty Principle
Sets a limit on how accurately certain pairs of properties (like position and momentum) can be known simultaneously.
Entanglement
Creates lasting correlations between particles, no matter how far apart.
Technology Powered by Quantum
From transistors to quantum computers, quantum mechanics underlies countless technologies.

Operators & Observables

Operators
Tools that act on the wavefunction to reveal measurable properties—observables—like position, energy, and spin.
Eigenvalues & Eigenstates
An eigenstate remains unchanged by an operator, yet returns a multiplier (eigenvalue)—the definite measurement.
Commutators
Measure the degree to which two operators can be swapped without affecting the outcome. If they don't commute, simultaneous measurement is limited—this underlies the uncertainty principle.
Time Evolution
Governed by the Schrödinger equation, with the Hamiltonian guiding how wavefunctions evolve. Though the wavefunction evolves deterministically, measurements remain probabilistic.
